Subtheme: Speculation

Overall questions:

1. Are there any patterns to vacancies and/or housing availability around the Greater Boston area?

The article about Boston’s ultra luxe “towers of wealth” reminded me about a recent trip to New York to visit a friend. As we strolled around Central Park, she remarked on the impossible to miss shadow of the Central Park Tower, adding that many of the high rises of Manhattan remain empty (not to mention prohibitively expensive for the average NYC resident). This reminded me of some videos I’d seen on the past on New York’s empty “Billionaires’ Row" which echoed many of the readings, explaining that financial capitalism in the 21st century has shifted in such a way that real estate has moved down the liquidity spectrum to become more like a stock or cash. The video also brought up a term coined by Jonny Aspen, a urban studies professor: “zombie urbanism”, used to describe a city that has a lot of sold housing units but aren’t lived in. Both from a material perspective and from a urban planning / urban studies perspective, having extremely expensive housing sitting empty in cities that are also in the throes of housing crises has always baffled me. As such, I’d like to explore vacancies (if such data is available) and patterns of availability in Boston.

2. What would be the potential effects of a transfer policy enactment? How do Mayor Wu and Governor Healey's respective proposals compare in the context of Boston?

Learning about speculation wasn’t just eye-opening — it also interested me in learning what can actually be done about the issue and ensuring greater housing equity. Both the Boston Globe ‘Towers of Wealth’ article and the Homes for Profit site mentioned transfer fees (taxes on a certain portion of a real estate property above a certain threshold) as a possible policy solution. A quick Google search informed me that there are actually two different transfer fee policies that are being floated in MA state legislation. I wanted to explore different aspects of these proposals. For example, where would a $2 million cutoff (Mayor Wu’s proposal) in Boston occur and what properties / who would it affect? What about Governor Healey’s proposal, with the first $1.0 million or county median price? (For context, Suffolk County’s median home sale in 2023 was $709,125, meaning Boston’s initial threshold would be set at $1.0 million.) And would these transfer fees actually target investor / private equity groups?

3. Are there any patterns to "flipping" in the Greater Boston area? Where does flipping primarily occur? Who does it affect?

A metaphor that stood out to me in the ProPublica article was how private equity firms act like corporate house flippers: “They seek deals on apartment buildings, slash costs or hike rents to boost income, then unload the buildings at a higher price.” But it’s clear that this isn’t just a metaphor — investor groups often do actually purchase properties and resell them for higher. Although the data can’t speak for the quality of the properties after flipping, we can figure out some important information about the flips themselves, such as where they often occur and the percentage price change. Gathering more information about potential price increases is especially crucial, given that Boston’s prohibitively high costs are an important aspect of the housing crisis.

Discoveries & Insights

Because many of my questions revolved around patterns in Boston’s housing (different neighborhoods, different types of housing properties, etc), I started by getting a general sense of the data. First, I created a chart showing the average proportions of Boston’s neighborhoods by race using the census data:

It’s important to note here that I took an aggregated average over the census data times — so these are averages from over 20 years rather than snapshots for certain times. This idea of aggregation vs. more granular breakdowns became a common thread throughout my charts. We can’t exactly view whether or not these neighborhoods have always looked the same racially across this time period, but given Boston’s history of redlining and racial segregation in housing, coupled with my own assumption that most people don’t move between different neighborhoods very often, we can still draw some important insights from the graph.

We can get a sense of what neighborhoods are predominantly Black (Mattapan, Hyde Park), white (Beacon Hill, South Boston Waterfront [from here on, I will refer to this neighborhood as Seaport]), Downtown), Asian (Chinatown), Hispanic (East Boston, Roxbury) and a mix of races (Dorchester, Roslindale). Because I was planning to use neighborhoods / zip codes for many of my later visualizations, it was helpful to get a general demographic breakdown of each neighborhood. I was also left with more questions about socioeconomic demographics — general income levels, poverty levels, education levels, etc. Though this data wasn’t included in our dataset, this could be worthwhile to explore in the future.

Next, I took a look at types of properties sold over time (excluding commercial, for our purposes of exploring housing). The data reflected trends that generally made sense, such as the dip in properties sold from 2008 - 2010 (Great Recession) and a dip in 2020 (COVID-19 pandemic). Apartments seemed to be the most commonly sold property across the entire span of the dataset, with condos and single and double family residences close behind. Notably, though the real estate market picked up in 2021, it’s been on a decline since then, with fewer properties being sold overall.

Because my theme was speculation, I took a look at corporate ownership rates over time. First, confirming what we already know, average corporate ownership of properties in Boston has risen almost exponentially over the past 20 years or so, growing from around 5% in 2004 to 25% in 2024. It seems the pandemic was an especially fruitful time for corporate takeovers, jumping up over 3% from 2019 to 2020.

I then wanted to explore how these corporate own rates were distributed among Boston’s neighborhoods over time, so I created a map comparing the average corporate own rates over a 16 year time span.

Note: try scrolling down the visualization and adjusting view at bottom to desktop for best results.

The areas of greatest financial activity and wealth (Downtown, Beacon Hill, Seaport) seemed to the first areas to have more corporate ownership activity, though in 2020 activity seemed to expand further to Allston, Brighton, etc. The map also corroborated the previous visualization’s story of greater ownership activity during and after the COVID-19 pandemic. It would be interesting to take a look into if there were any pandemic-related policy or economic changes during the time that made it especially accessible for corporate takeovers. I ran into a slight data quality issue here, since it seems that we’re missing 1 or 2 zip codes for neighborhoods, so there are a few missing areas on the map.

However, my focus on corporate owned properties led to want to explore if other types of ownerships have gone down as a result. I compared corporate owned properties to owner occupied properties in each neighborhood.

Again, across all neighborhoods, we see the same pattern of corporate ownership ticking up over time, though with various amounts of ownership. Some neighborhoods, like Fenway and Seaport, had a relatively high percentage of corporate ownership, (36% and 38%, respectively), while more residential neighborhoods like Roslindale and West Roxbury had high ownership occupancy rates and low corporate ownership, suggesting that there are certain areas of Boston that are highly segmented in their usage (residential vs. commercial / business, etc), and that mixed-use areas of Boston seem to be of greatest interest to corporations. Primarily BIPOC-occupied neighborhoods (see first visualization), like Dorchester, Mattapan, and Hyde Park, had relatively low rates of corporate ownership, though there have been slow increases over the years. Given more data, it would be interesting to explore what other stakeholders are potentially involved in property ownership across Boston (eg. federal land, state-owned land, etc).

After getting a general sense of the data, I wanted to explore my first question about vacancy and availability around Boston. This was where I ran into another data issue: the vacancy data was not updated on a census-to-census basis so I couldn’t look at how vacancy rates changed over time.

Nevertheless, I was still able to get a sense of the aggregated vacancy rates as of 2024 in each neighborhood, and compare it to the average corporate ownership rate map from before. Seaport is the neighborhood with the highest vacancy rate, which, given its high corporate ownership rate from the other visualizations, suggests a potential correlation. The map overall does seem to hint that higher corporate ownership rates correlate with higher vacancy rates, and that higher vacancy rates occur in the areas closest to the harbour / center of Boston.

But I also realized that the different neighborhoods of Boston may have different population densities which may have been driving the differences in vacancy rates.

Though we didn’t have the data for population density, I decided to compare total population in neighborhoods as of 2024 and their vacancy rates. Interestingly, the population of neighborhoods didn’t seem to be a big driver into vacancy rates, which leads me to think that geography and usage of different neighborhoods seem to drive pricing and subsequent affordability, which in turn affects vacancy rates.

I decided to investigate my theory about pricing and took a look at median sales of properties in 2022.

Again, we see that the usual culprits near the center of Boston (Beacon Hill, Downtown, Back Bay, and Seaport) tend to have the highest median prices. I took the median because averages can be heavily affected by outliers, and I didn’t want one or two multimillion mansion purchase in a neighborhood to throw off my numbers. Overall, a picture was beginning to emerge about corporate ownerships, vacancy, and prices — higher corporate ownerships correlated with higher vacancies and higher prices.

The visualizations were all pointing to a clear increase in housing prices over time, which was a nice segway into my third question surrounding the increase in property flipping (another consequence of increased corporate ownership, as detailed in the ProPublica article) and how flipping affects affordability.

I started by creating a simple area chart of median price difference percentages (defined in the metadata as “the percentage change in the price of a property between consecutive transactions”) over time. Again, no surprise that 2008-2012 had a low price difference percentage, but I was quite shocked at just how much the percentages ballooned over the following decade, with the median price difference percentage almost doubling from 2000 to 2024. This points to just how hard it’s become for people to break into the housing market in Boston. Indeed, it’s highly likely that someone who bought their house 15 years ago would not be able to afford it today.

This chart led to me a little detour to explore changes in pricing over time in general. This led to perhaps my most damning visualization of my entire exploration. I compared median price vs median average annual value over time:

The visual speaks for itself — median prices have skyrocketed over the past 24 years, almost tripling, while the average annual value (sum of transaction price / total years elapsed in the dataset) has barely even grown, hence the stark difference between the 2 lines over time. This suggests that the return value of real estate transactions has not at all kept pace with the housing market itself, pointing to the inflated pricing and inefficiencies in Boston’s real estate market. If there’s any visualization in this assignment that I would call the most important, it would be this one, because it illustrates just how much must be done to make housing affordable again.

Returning to the question of property flipping, I created a map comparing the buy side flip proportions (buy side meaning the purchaser has the intent to flip the property) and sell side flip proportions (sell side meaning the transaction was a flipped property) from 2020 to 2022.

It seems that this dataset is missing flip information for a significant number of neighborhoods. We still get a sense for which neighborhoods seem to experience the highest number of flips (defined in our data as a purchase and selling of a property within 2 years), namely Longwood and Downtown.

In terms of the buy side flip map, we see that though proportions of flips are relatively low through out the neighborhoods that are represented but the missing data in more diverse and/or lower-income neighborhoods made me think of the link between flipping or redevelopment and gentrification. This could be a great topic for further research.

The maps focusing on buying vs selling made me interested in who is conducting the buying and selling across the market. Of course, we have note that we’re limited to just the buyers and sellers who were flagged in the dataset.

First, I created a pivot column of the different buyers of properties — we have bank buyers, BUS buyers (Buyer is an INC, CORP, CO, INC), government buyers, LLCs, trusts, and GSE (Fannie Mae and Freddie Mac). Then I looked at the median price of properties bought, split across the different buyers. There may be potential data issues, with GSE and government information exhibiting the most missingness. At the same time, there may be some other reason why government / GSE buyers were active in certain years (recession related buyouts, etc), so further investigation is needed. One thing that does seem clear though is that LLCs seem to be able to afford more expensive properties compared to other entities and their buying power seems to have increased over the years.

This becomes more obvious when looking at the average prices by buyer (also a good illustration of why I chose to use mostly use median when it came to pricing!) — LLCs buy much more expensive properties, averaging above $5 million, compared to other buyers. Using the visualization of types of properties sold, we can infer that most of these properties are either apartment or condo buildings.

I repeated a similar process with the different sellers, creating a pivot column with banks, BUS sellers (INC, CORP, CO, INC), government entities, GSE (Freddie Mac & Fannie Mae), LLCs, LLPs, and trusts. We see similar potential missingness among sellers, again an issue to flag for more investigation. This graph reveals that the most powerful sellers changed over time, going from banks to corporations, to government entities, and then back to corporations today. The rise of corporate sellers (orange line) in both the buyer and seller charts support our earlier visualizations investigating corporate ownership rates in Boston. We see that in our seller chart specifically that the properties that are being sold by corporations have higher median prices than properties that they buy, which also points to potential flipping or other speculative practices that price out low to middle income buyers.

Unfortunately, the calculations seem off so I don’t want to draw any conclusions from these visualizations. However, if they were working, they could give us insight into whether or not corporations / LLCs would be affected by the transfer fee policies.
